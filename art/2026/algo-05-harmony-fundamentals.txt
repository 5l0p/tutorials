Algorithmic Composition - Part 5: Harmony Fundamentals
2026-01-15


A melody generator from Parts 3 and 4 outputs notes one at a time. Add simultaneous
notes — two, three, or four pitches sounding together — and you have harmony. Harmony
is not just decoration. The relationship between pitches in a chord, and between
consecutive chords, determines whether the music feels tense or relaxed, moving or
stable, resolved or suspended. An algorithm that generates chord progressions needs
a model of these relationships.



That model starts with physics. The frequency ratios between pitches determine their
acoustic interaction: some ratios produce simple, consonant relationships; others
produce complex, beating interactions that the ear perceives as tension. This is
not a cultural convention — it follows from the overtone series and the mechanics of
how the cochlea resolves frequency differences.

The Overtone Series


A vibrating string produces not just its fundamental frequency f, but also overtones at
integer multiples: 2f, 3f, 4f, 5f, and so on. These overtones are always present in
any naturally produced pitch. The first few intervals they define:



Fundamental (1f) → octave (2f):   ratio 2:1
Octave (2f) → fifth (3f):         ratio 3:2
Fifth (3f) → fourth (4f):         ratio 4:3
Fourth (4f) → major third (5f):   ratio 5:4
Major third (5f) → minor third (6f): ratio 6:5



The simpler the ratio, the more consonant the interval. A perfect fifth (3:2) is more
consonant than a major third (5:4), which is more consonant than a minor third (6:5),
which is more consonant than a tritone (45:32 in just intonation — not a simple ratio).



Consonance is not binary. It exists on a spectrum from the unison (1:1, maximally
consonant) through the octave, fifth, fourth, third, sixth, second, and seventh to the
tritone (maximally dissonant in Western tonal music). The tritone is the interval
exactly halfway around the chromatic circle — six semitones — and its lack of a simple
integer ratio makes it acoustically unstable.


Equal Temperament: The Compromise


Just intonation uses exact integer ratios. A perfect fifth is exactly 3:2 = 1.5000.
In twelve-tone equal temperament, a fifth is 2^(7/12) = 1.4983 — 1.96 cents flat of
just. The difference is small enough that most listeners cannot detect it in isolation.



The reason for the compromise: just intonation produces slightly different tunings for
the same note in different keys. A keyboard instrument tuned just in C will be audibly
out of tune in F# major. Equal temperament spreads the mistuning evenly across all keys,
making every key equally (slightly) out of tune and enabling free modulation.



For algorithmic composition with MIDI, you always work in equal temperament. The
consonance relationships from just intonation are approximately preserved.


Constructing Triads


A triad is three pitches: a root, a third above, and a fifth above. The four basic
triad types, specified as semitone intervals from root:



local triad_types = {
    major      = {0, 4, 7},   -- root, major third (4 semitones), perfect fifth (7)
    minor      = {0, 3, 7},   -- root, minor third (3), perfect fifth (7)
    diminished = {0, 3, 6},   -- root, minor third (3), diminished fifth (6)
    augmented  = {0, 4, 8},   -- root, major third (4), augmented fifth (8)
}



To build a C major triad rooted at MIDI 60: add offsets {0, 4, 7} to get {60, 64, 67}
(C4, E4, G4). To build A minor rooted at 69: add {0, 3, 7} to get {69, 72, 76}
(A4, C5, E5).



The perfect fifth (7 semitones, present in both major and minor) is the interval closest
to a just 3:2 ratio. It is the most acoustically stable interval after the octave, which
is why triads built on it feel grounded. The difference between major and minor is the
third: 4 semitones (close to 5:4 just major third) versus 3 semitones (close to 6:5 just
minor third).


Diatonic Triads


In a major scale, you can build a triad on each of the seven degrees using only scale
tones (no chromatic alterations). The triad type is determined by the scale:



-- Diatonic triad types in a major scale
-- I=major, II=minor, III=minor, IV=major, V=major, VI=minor, VII=diminished
local major_diatonic = {
    major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, diminished
}
-- (using indices 1-7 for degrees)



In Lua, build the diatonic triads for a given root and scale:



local pitch = require("pitch")

local function diatonic_triads(root, scale, triad_types)
    local n = #scale
    local triads = {}
    for d = 1, n do
        -- The three scale degrees of the triad: d, d+2, d+4 (with wrap)
        local function sc_pitch(deg)
            -- deg is 1-based, possibly  n
            local i = ((deg - 1) % n) + 1
            local octave_bump = math.floor((deg - 1) / n) * 12
            return root + scale[i] + octave_bump
        end
        triads[d] = {
            sc_pitch(d),
            sc_pitch(d + 2),
            sc_pitch(d + 4),
        }
    end
    return triads
end

-- C major, root C4 = 60
local triads = diatonic_triads(60, pitch.scales.major, triad_types)

for d, t in ipairs(triads) do
    print(string.format("Degree %d: %s %s %s",
        d, pitch.name(t[1]), pitch.name(t[2]), pitch.name(t[3])))
end



Output:



Degree 1: C4 E4 G4
Degree 2: D4 F4 A4
Degree 3: E4 G4 B4
Degree 4: F4 A4 C5
Degree 5: G4 B4 D5
Degree 6: A4 C5 E5
Degree 7: B4 D5 F5


Roman Numeral Analysis


Western music theory names chords by their scale degree using Roman numerals: I, II, III,
IV, V, VI, VII. Uppercase means major quality; lowercase means minor or diminished. In C
major: I (C major), ii (D minor), iii (E minor), IV (F major), V (G major), vi (A minor),
vii° (B diminished).



The numbering is key-independent: I is always the tonic triad of whatever key you are in.
This lets you describe a chord progression as I–IV–V–I and have it apply in any key by
substituting the appropriate root. In Lua, Roman numeral analysis means working with
degree indices (1–7) rather than absolute pitch classes. This is why the algorithms
in Parts 3 and 4 work in scale degrees and only convert to MIDI at output time.


Harmonic Function


The three primary triads — I (tonic), IV (subdominant), V (dominant) — have distinct
harmonic functions that transcend their specific pitches:



Tonic (I, and by extension iii, vi): Stability. Home. The ear's default resting
state. A piece can end here. Tension does not need to resolve when you are on a tonic chord.



Subdominant (IV, and ii): Movement away from tonic. A mild raising of tension, a
sense of departure. Often precedes the dominant. IV–V–I is the cadential archetype.



Dominant (V, and vii°): Strong tension, strong pull toward resolution. The V chord
contains the leading tone (degree 7), which has a strong gravitational pull upward to the
tonic. V–I is the strongest cadential motion in tonal music.



Functional labels T (tonic), S (subdominant), and D (dominant) abstract away the specific
chord and capture the harmonic role. The Markov chain in Part 6 operates on T, S, D —
not on chord degrees — so the progression grammar is key-independent.


Modal Interchange


Modal interchange borrows chords from the parallel minor (or other modes) without fully
modulating. In C major, the parallel minor is C minor. Its diatonic chords include
bVII (Bb major), bVI (Ab major), and iv (F minor) — none of which appear in C major.
Borrowing bVII into a C major progression adds colour without abandoning the key.



In Lua, represent borrowed chords as additional entries with a flag:



local chord_db = {
    -- diatonic major chords
    [1]  = {root_offset=0,  type="major",      function_="T"},
    [2]  = {root_offset=2,  type="minor",      function_="S"},
    [3]  = {root_offset=4,  type="minor",      function_="T"},
    [4]  = {root_offset=5,  type="major",      function_="S"},
    [5]  = {root_offset=7,  type="major",      function_="D"},
    [6]  = {root_offset=9,  type="minor",      function_="T"},
    [7]  = {root_offset=11, type="diminished", function_="D"},
    -- borrowed from parallel minor
    [8]  = {root_offset=10, type="major",      function_="D", borrowed=true},  -- bVII
    [9]  = {root_offset=8,  type="major",      function_="S", borrowed=true},  -- bVI
    [10] = {root_offset=5,  type="minor",      function_="S", borrowed=true},  -- iv
}

-- Build absolute MIDI pitches for a chord entry
local function build_chord(entry, root)
    local cr = root + entry.root_offset
    local t  = triad_types[entry.type]
    return {cr + t[1], cr + t[2], cr + t[3]}
end



The chord_db table is the data structure Part 6 uses when generating
progressions. Part 7 uses it to look up the chord tones for voice leading.


Summary


Consonance follows from the overtone series: simple integer frequency ratios produce
stable intervals (octave 2:1, fifth 3:2, major third 5:4); complex ratios produce
tension (tritone 45:32). Equal temperament approximates just intonation closely enough
for practical use while enabling free key modulation. Triads are three-note chords built
by stacking thirds; the four types (major, minor, diminished, augmented) differ in the
size of those thirds. Roman numeral analysis names chords by scale degree; harmonic
function (T, S, D) captures their role in creating and releasing tension. Modal
interchange borrows chords from parallel modes for colour. These are the data structures
that the chord progression generator in Part 6 and the voice leading algorithm in
Part 7 operate on.
