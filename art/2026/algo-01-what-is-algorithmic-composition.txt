Algorithmic Composition - Part 1: What Is Algorithmic Composition?
2026-06-01


Building a drum machine from first principles (Series 9) and recreating Elektron synthesis
engines in Pure Data (Series 10) answers one question: how do you produce a sound? It
leaves the deeper question open: what sound do you produce, and when? Choosing notes by
hand works for a composed piece. For a generative instrument — something that runs
unattended, evolves over time, and never repeats exactly — you need to compose the rules
that choose the notes, not the notes themselves.



That shift, from composing notes to composing rule systems, is algorithmic composition.
It ranges from loosely probabilistic (throw a coin to decide whether to play a note) to
tightly structured (derive every pitch from a cellular automaton seeded at startup). Most
musically interesting work sits in the middle: systems with enough randomness to be
unpredictable and enough structure to be coherent.



This series builds that middle ground. By the end you will have a self-generating patch
in Pure Data, driven by Lua scripts, that produces melodies, chord progressions, and
rhythms using six techniques: random walks, Markov chains, harmonic voice leading,
L-system grammars, cellular automata, and Euclidean rhythm distribution. All of them
feed the synthesis engines from Series 9 and 10 with note data.

The Spectrum: Stochastic to Deterministic


Fully stochastic composition picks every parameter independently at random. The result is
statistically flat — no phrase shapes, no tendency for resolution, no sense of direction.
It is unpredictable because nothing constrains consecutive choices, which is also why it
is uninteresting to listen to for long.



Fully deterministic composition derives everything from a fixed rule with no randomness.
A strict canon is deterministic: the second voice is an exact transposition of the first
at a fixed delay. Predictable, but not generative in the sense of producing varied output.



Between those poles:



Constrained random — random choices within a harmonic or rhythmic framework
(e.g. random pitches, but only from a major pentatonic scale).
Stochastic process — random but with memory or momentum (Brownian motion,
Markov chains), so consecutive outputs are correlated.
Grammar-based — a rule system (L-system, cellular automaton) that expands a
seed deterministically into complex output, but where the seed itself can be random.



The most musically useful systems combine all three: a grammar generates large-scale
structure, a stochastic process generates melodic contour within that structure,
and harmonic constraints filter the output to in-key pitches.


Three Historical Reference Points


ILLIAC Suite (1957) — Hiller and Isaacson programmed the ILLIAC I computer at
the University of Illinois to compose string quartet music using Markov chains and
rule-based filtering. The system generated candidate notes and rejected those that
violated species counterpoint rules (no parallel fifths, stepwise bass motion, and so
on). This is still the clearest demonstration that style can be encoded as a set of
acceptance/rejection rules applied to stochastic output.



Music for Airports (1978) — Brian Eno recorded individual notes and looped them on
tape at different lengths. Because the loop lengths are mutually incommensurable (no
common period), the combinations never exactly repeat. This is the simplest generative
system imaginable: no algorithm, just the arithmetic of coprime loop lengths. It
demonstrates that perceived complexity can emerge from very simple rules, and that
"generative" does not require a computer.



The Krell Patch (1967) — Morton Subotnick's Buchla modular patch for "Silver
Apples of the Moon" used the output envelope of one oscillator to gate a second,
which in turn gated a third. The system had no clock: each sound triggered the next
with a delay determined by its own envelope time. Randomness entered via the
voltage-controlled oscillators drifting over time. The result was self-sustaining and
constantly evolving. Part 11 of this series implements a Krell-style architecture in
Pure Data.


Three Problems Every Generative System Must Solve


Pitch selection — what note to play. The system must choose pitches that form
coherent melodic phrases and stay within a harmonic framework. Parts 2–4 address this:
encoding pitch as MIDI numbers, building scale tables, generating melodies with Brownian
walks and Markov chains.



Harmonic coherence — how simultaneous or successive pitches relate. A sequence of
random pitches from a major scale sounds in-key but not harmonically structured. Parts 5–7
address chord construction, progression generation with Markov chains over harmonic
functions, and voice leading to move smoothly between chords.



Rhythm — when to play notes and for how long. Parts 9–10 address Euclidean rhythm
distribution (which produces asymmetric patterns found in world music) and cellular
automaton rhythms (which produce evolving patterns from a fixed rule).


Why Lua for Generation, Pure Data for Synthesis


The drum machine in Series 9 already established this split. Lua holds the pattern data
and parameter presets; Pure Data holds the signal graph. The same boundary works here.



Generation logic — Markov chains, L-system rewriters, Euclidean patterns, voice leading
calculations — is table manipulation and arithmetic. Lua handles this cleanly with no
overhead.



Synthesis and real-time audio — oscillators, filters, envelopes, MIDI I/O — runs
sample-accurately in Pure Data. Lua cannot run at audio rate. The two communicate via
[send] / [receive] pairs: Lua sends a list of MIDI note numbers
at note-event rate; Pure Data routes them to the correct synthesis voice.



The interface between the two is always a Pd message: a bang, a float, or a list. The
Lua script produces note data; a Pd [receive] object picks it up and routes
it to an oscillator or sequencer.


Series Overview


Part 2 — Pitch as Numbers: MIDI note numbers, equal temperament, scale tables in Lua, the quantise function that maps any pitch to the nearest in-scale note.
Part 3 — Random Walks and Brownian Motion: Why pure random pitch is musically useless; Brownian motion as a walk through pitch space; step size, boundaries, and fractional Brownian motion with the Hurst exponent.
Part 4 — Markov Chains for Melody: Transition probability matrices over scale degrees; sampling from a distribution; rhythm chains; combining pitch and rhythm into a melody generator.
Part 5 — Harmony Fundamentals: The overtone series and why certain intervals are consonant; triads as stacked scale degrees; tonic, subdominant, and dominant function.
Part 6 — Generating Chord Progressions: Markov chains over harmonic function rather than individual chords; modal interchange for colour.
Part 7 — Voice Leading: Choosing voicings that minimise pitch movement between chords; nearest-neighbour assignment; detecting and avoiding parallel fifths and octaves.
Part 8 — L-Systems: String-rewriting grammars as melody generators; self-similar structure at multiple time scales; turtle-graphics interpretation of symbol strings.
Part 9 — Cellular Automata: 1D elementary automata (Rule 30, Rule 110) as pitch and rhythm generators; Conway's Game of Life for evolving harmonic density.
Part 10 — Euclidean Rhythms: Bjorklund's algorithm for distributing beats evenly; connections to world-music rhythmic patterns; polyrhythms from coprime period lengths.
Part 11 — Putting It All Together: A Krell-style self-generating patch wiring the Monomachine VO and SuperWave engines (from Series 10) and the drum machine (from Series 9) into a single autonomous system.


Summary


Algorithmic composition replaces hand-chosen notes with rule systems that generate note
data. The spectrum runs from pure stochastic (memoryless random) to fully deterministic
(strict rule, no randomness); musically useful systems combine both. The three core
problems are pitch selection, harmonic coherence, and rhythm. In this series, Lua solves
all three algorithmically and delivers results to Pure Data via messages; Pure Data
synthesises the audio using the engines built in Series 9 and 10.
