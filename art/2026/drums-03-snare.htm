Drums - Part 3: Snare Drum Synthesis in Pure Data

A snare drum has two acoustic components: the batter head (which produces a tonal thwack) and the snare wires (which produce the characteristic buzz and sizzle). This article builds both from scratch in Pure Data, adding the dual feedback delay rattle that makes a snare sound like it has wires rather than being a simple drum hit.

What You Will Learn

  • Two sine oscillators for the snare body tone with independent pitch sweeps
  • SID-style noise: hard-clipped and sample-rate-reduced for a digital buzz character
  • Dual filter paths on the noise: lowpass (body) and highpass (snap/sizzle)
  • A resonant bandpass body filter for tonal shaping
  • Dual short feedback delay lines for snare wire rattle simulation
  • Two-stage distortion
  • X/Y mapping: body tone vs snare tail

The Snare Body — Two Sines

A real snare head resonates at multiple modes. The dominant modes for a 14" snare are approximately 180 Hz (fundamental) and 280 Hz (second mode). Model these with two sine oscillators, each with its own pitch sweep and amplitude envelope: 

; Sine A — fundamental body tone (~150–250 Hz)
[r $0-sineAFreq]   → [f]    ; base frequency
[r $0-sineAEnv]    → [f]    ; pitch sweep amount
[r $0-sineADecay]  → [f]    ; pitch sweep duration
[r $0-sineAAmpDec] → [f]    ; amplitude decay

; On trigger:
; freq vline~: peakFreq 0, baseFreq $sineADecay
; amp vline~: 1 2, 0 $sineAAmpDec 2

[osc~]   ← freq vline~
|
[*~]     ← amp vline~
|
[*~ $sineAMix]

; Sine B — second mode (~250–400 Hz, shorter decay)
; Same structure, typically 1.5–2× SineA frequency, 40% shorter decay

SID Noise

"SID noise" refers to the lo-fi digital buzz of a noise source run through a bit-crusher or rate reducer — named after the Commodore 64's SID chip noise register. For a snare it provides gritty texture that distinguishes digital percussion from a pure sine body.

In Pure Data, approximate it by hard-clipping white noise and optionally running it through a sample-and-hold at a reduced rate: 

; SID noise approximation:
[noise~]
|
[clip~ -0.15 0.15]     ; hard saturation → buzzy square-wave texture
|
[*~ 6.67]              ; restore approximate amplitude (1/0.15)
|
[samphold~ $0-clockRate]  ; sample-and-hold: reduces effective sample rate
                           ; $0-clockRate is a signal from [phasor~ f]:
                           ; [phasor~ $clockFreq] → drives samphold reset

Without [samphold~], the hard-clipped noise sounds like distorted white noise. With it (clock rate 0.3–0.5 × sample rate), it takes on a rougher, more digital character. For most snare patches, clock rates of 0.5–0.8 work well.

Implementing sample-and-hold in Pd: there is no built-in [samphold~] in vanilla Pd, but it can be approximated with a [phasor~] driving a comparator that gates a [sig~] latch, or with the else library's [sh~]. Alternatively, simply apply a lowpass filter with a very low cutoff to approximate rate reduction: 

; Simple rate-reduction approximation via lowpass:
[noise~]
|
[clip~ -0.15 0.15]
|
[lop~ $0-clockHz]      ; low cutoff (e.g. 4000 Hz) for lo-fi character

Dual Noise Filter Paths

The SID noise is split into two parallel filter paths, each with its own envelope: 

SID noise bus
    |
    ├── [lop~ $filterACutoff $filterAQ]    ; LPF path: warm, bassy noise
    │       |
    │   [vline~]  LPF decay envelope (snare body/tail)
    │       |
    │   [*~ $sidNoiseLPFMix]
    │
    └── [hip~ $filterBCutoff $filterBQ]   ; HPF path: bright snap/sizzle
            |
        [vline~]  HPF decay envelope (shorter = tighter snap)
            |
        [*~ $sidNoiseHPFMix]

The LPF path (with a slow decay) provides the snare's body and tail. The HPF path (with a faster decay) provides the initial snap and sizzle. Adjusting the ratio of these two paths moves from a dead dry snare (mostly HPF, short) to a lush full snare (both paths, longer decays).

Resonant Body Filter

A bandpass filter applied to the mixed sine+noise signal adds resonance and tonal focus, simulating the frequency response of the drum shell: 

mixed sines bus
    |
[bp~ $bodyCutoff $bodyQ]   ; resonant bandpass
    |
[*~ $bodyMix]              ; mix level (0 = bypass, 1 = full body filter)

Setting $bodyCutoff to 200–400 Hz and $bodyQ to 2–4 adds a warm, woody resonance. Higher Q values (8–12) produce a pitched, "rim-shot" quality.

Dual Delay Rattle

Real snare wires vibrate sympathetically for tens of milliseconds after the batter head is struck. Model this with two short, filtered feedback delay lines in parallel: 

; Delay line 1:
[delwrite~ $0-d1 200]          ; 200 ms maximum delay buffer

; Read with feedback:
[delread~ $0-d1 $delayTime1]   ; $delayTime1: 15–60 ms
    |
[lop~ $delFeedFilter1]         ; lowpass in feedback path (rolls off high freq)
    |
[*~ $feedback1]                ; feedback level (0.3–0.6)
→ back into delwrite input (add to dry signal going in)

[delread~ $0-d1 $delayTime1]   ; take output separately for mix
    |
[*~ $delayLevel1]              ; output level

; Delay line 2: identical structure, different time
[delwrite~ $0-d2 200]
...

In Pd, implement with [$0-d1] to namespace the delay buffer per instance: 

[inlet~]
|
[+~]              ← feedback return
|
[delwrite~ $0-d1 200]

[delread~ $0-d1 30]
|
[lop~ 8000]       ; damp highs in feedback
|
[*~ 0.45]         ; feedback coefficient
|
[+~] (back to inlet adder above)

[delread~ $0-d1 30]
|
[*~ $delayLevel1] ; to output mix

Two delay lines tuned to slightly different times (e.g. 28 ms and 41 ms) produce a richer, more random-feeling rattle than a single delay. Longer delay times (80–100 ms) make a very loose, sloppy snare; shorter times (15–25 ms) make a tight, crisp wire buzz.

Distortion

The snare model uses two distortion stages: 

mixed signal
    |
[clip~ -$dist1 $dist1]     ; first stage: hard clip (0.3–1.0 threshold)
    |
[clip~ -$dist2 $dist2]     ; second stage: softer clip

Two cascaded clippers with different thresholds produce a more complex saturation character than a single stage. The first stage creates harmonics; the second round off the harshest edges. For most snare patches: first stage 0.4–0.7, second stage 0.6–1.0.

Complete Signal Graph

On $0-trigger:
  → fire SineA pitch + amp vline~
  → fire SineB pitch + amp vline~
  → fire LPF noise decay vline~
  → fire HPF noise decay vline~
  → fire output envelope vline~

Signal path:
[sineA] *~ [ampA] *~ [sineAMix] ─┐
[sineB] *~ [ampB] *~ [sineBMix] ─┤ sines bus
                                  │
    [bp~ bodyCutoff bodyQ] ← sines bus
    [*~ bodyMix] ──────────────── ─┤

[SID noise]
   ├─ [lop~ filterA] *~ [LPF env] *~ [lpfMix] ─┤
   └─ [hip~ filterB] *~ [HPF env] *~ [hpfMix] ─┤ mix bus

[transient click noise] *~ [3ms env] *~ [clickMix] ─┤

mix bus
  │
[clip~ dist1] [clip~ dist2]    ; two-stage distortion
  │
[+~ delay1 delay2]             ; add delay rattle
  │
[*~ outEnv]                    ; output envelope
  │
[*~ velocity]
  │
[outlet~]

Build Order

  1. Single [osc~] at 180 Hz with amplitude decay → verify the body thud.
  2. Add [noise~] through [lop~] with its own shorter decay → snare character begins.
  3. Add the HPF noise path → snap emerges.
  4. Add SID noise approximation (hard clip) → gritty texture.
  5. Add Sine B (250 Hz).
  6. Add bandpass body filter.
  7. Add one delay line (30 ms, 0.4 feedback) → rattle appears.
  8. Add second delay line.
  9. Add two-stage clip distortion.
  10. Wire output envelope and velocity.
  11. Wire all $0- receivers and test presets.

Summary

The snare combines two sine oscillators (body modes) with SID-style noise split into a warm LPF path (tail) and a bright HPF path (snap). A resonant bandpass filter shapes the body character. Two short filtered feedback delays simulate snare wire rattle. Two-stage distortion adds grit. X maps to body tone frequency (dark→bright); Y maps to noise decay (tight→sizzling). The same extend-decay envelope pattern from Part 1 drives both the sine amplitude and noise paths independently.
